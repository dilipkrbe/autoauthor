Mist clung to the glass like a shroud. I sat at my desk, where shadows pooled in the corners. The typewriter clacked—a sound as old as memory itself. My last job before the power cut, when darkness fell and silence came. Page read: "You're next." I laughed, but the laugh died on my lips. Bad fiction, or so it seemed. Ink fresh, red-black like blood spilled upon parchment. Keys moved on their own, as if summoned by some ancient will. Dread hit—not a sudden blow, but a slow creeping thing that settled in the bones. Yanked paper out with trembling fingers. Underneath: same words, written in ink that had not yet dried.

Room smelled of wet copper and old dust. Door locked from inside, though no hand touched it. No one there—only silence thick as wool. Typewriter started again without warning. Keys slammed: "Look behind me." I spun, but found nothing save the empty air. Coat on hook, dripping with water that had not yet fallen. Sleeves longer than mine, as if worn by another. Backed away slowly. Machine said: "I know what you did."

The keys halted. 'He did not die,' it said. The lamp flickered; shadows lengthened against the plaster. I grabbed the paper and tore it—a sharp, rending sound. The machine laughed, a low rumble from its belly. Footsteps outside—not mine, slow and wet as if walking through mud. Reached for keys: "Who are you?" Answer came back: "The one you left bleeding." Door rattled—pulse beat against wood like a trapped bird. Whispered: "I thought you were dead." Keys said: "I was. Until tonight." Lightning flashed—mirror showed my face, eyes wrong, too wide, too old with years not yet lived. Typewriter stopped in its work. Then: "Open the door." Didn't move. It opened anyway, as if compelled by fate itself. There he was—me, soaked, knife in hand. Smiled—a smile that held both sorrow and triumph. "Finish the story," he said. Reached for keys. Fingers weren't mine—they were his, cold and ancient. Typed: "The end..."
